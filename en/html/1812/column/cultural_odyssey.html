Fireballs, Fruit and Festivities: Why New Year Rocks, Wherever You Are

Fireballs, Fruit and Festivities: Why New Year Rocks, Wherever You Are

By Tim Alper(Journalist)

December 31 is the most bittersweet day on the calendar – saying goodbye to the year gone by can be hard. But welcoming in a whole new year can also fill us with a new lease of hope. It is thus unsurprising that this significant, emotion-filled date is marked with colorful celebrations and vivid traditions in countries all over the world.

In Latin America, Panamanians fete the New Year with muñecos, effigies of political leaders, cartoon characters or celebrities who made a mark on the year that passed. The muñecos are stuffed with firecrackers and paraded about towns during the daytime. At the stroke of midnight, the effigies are set alight, creating a mighty cacophony as the firecrackers all ignite at once. The symbolism is rich here: The noisy explosions are thought to ward off evil spirits.

For Brazilians, who live in the Southern Hemisphere, December 31 is the middle of summer, so most folk celebrate with a late-night trip to the beach. Some send out floating candle offerings to a sea goddess named Iemanjá. If your candle drifts out of sight, it is said that Iemanjá will grant your wishes. But if the waves wash the offering back, you are out of luck!

Great Balls of Fire

In Europe, most people head out into the cold New Year’s Eve night air to watch spectacular firework displays. And in some countries, this is the year’s biggest party of all – with wild celebrations that mesmerize visitors.

The famous Hogmonay celebrations in Scotland’s Stonehaven involve revelers taking to the street to swing around giant fireballs on metal chains. These blazing balls are made of chicken wire, filled with burning sticks and rags; they light up the night like roaring dragons.

Fireball-swingers begin a procession at the stroke of midnight, walking from the town hall and all the way down to the harbor. If the fireballs are still alight when the procession reaches the seafront, tradition requires one of the group to hurl a flaming fireball into the cold, dark sea waters. The fire is thought to stave off evil spirits, and the spectacular tradition, which always brings in plenty of visitors from every part of the world, likely dates back to pagan times.

Fabulous Fruit

Asians also like to fete the New Year in style. Revelers in the Philippines, for instance, gather 12 different varieties of round fruit ahead of December 31. They then display these collections in their homes ahead of the midnight countdown.

As well as being a fun food-related challenge, this tradition is also laced with symbolism – each of the fruit varieties represents one of the months of the coming year. The fruit’s round form represents the circle of life, and the shape of coins – which are thought to be associated with good fortune. Filipinos also fill their pockets with coins as the last hour of the year approaches. Many even don polka dot designs in the hope that fortune will shine upon them in the year to come.

The Chinese, meanwhile, avoid sweeping on New Year’s Eve, fearing that over-keen broom-wielders could clean away all the good luck from the coming year. The Chinese also race to let off fireworks on the very second after midnight. The first person to let off a firework is thought to gain the most luck!

No matter which part of the planet you find yourself in on December 31, 2018, ushering in the New Year is bound to be a vibrant carnival of gleeful fun, culture and tradition – one that few other days can hold a candle to.

By Tim Alper(Journalist)

Tim Alper is a British journalist who has lived in Seoul for 11 years. He has contributed to publications such as The Guardian, The Jewish Chronicle, Joongang Ilbo, Weekly Chosun and Korean Air’s Morning Calm. He is also the author of the book Bananas & Couscous and the co-author of Have Fork, Will Travel.
