How proposed tariffs could affect LPG, bulker, and box ships

Market Report

How proposed tariffs could affect LPG, bulker, and box ships

Greg Miller,
senior editor of Fairplay,
part of IHS Markit

One week, the world appears on the brink of a full-fledged trade war. The next, tensions ease. The week after, they rekindle. Throughout it all, shipping sits in the crosshairs, its public equities whipsawed back and forth.

Depending on how the tariff saga plays out, owners and operators in multiple vessel categories could face consequences. In some cases, there is a definite effect from tariffs that have already been enacted: for bulkers and multipurpose vessels carrying aluminium and steel to the United States; medium-range product tankers transporting ethanol from America to China; and container ships carrying fruits, nuts, and wine from the United States to China.

In other cases, the shipping effect remains hypothetical, because tariffs have been threatened but not yet enacted. The key vessel categories at future risk are liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) carriers – particularly very large gas carriers (VLGCs) – transporting propane from the US Gulf to China; bulk carriers transporting soyabeans; and container ships carrying more than a thousand product types.

The case of propane highlights just how disconnected tariff politics is from the basic logic of transport economics. If the United States moves ahead with its plan to impose USD50 billion in tariffs on Chinese goods in retaliation for alleged intellectual property (IP) theft, China has vowed to respond with its own USD50 billion of tariffs, including 25% levies on US exports of propane and other products.

“This would hurt the Chinese, not the US,” explained IHS Markit senior director Scott Gray, noting that most of the propane sent to China is purchased under ‘take or pay’ agreements. “If they don’t lift cargoes, they still have to pay a [cancellation] fee. If tariffs are imposed, we may see additional [cargo] cancellations on the Gulf Coast, but [US propane sellers] Enterprise and Targa will still get their money.”

The alternative would be for China to buy propane in the Middle East, which would involve a much shorter sailing distance, a negative for vessel demand. In addition, more cargo cancellations in the US Gulf would lead to a longer queue of unemployed VLGCs available for spot voyages, yet another dampener for rates.

However, two countervailing factors could limit LPG shipping downside. First, most VLGCs leaving the US Gulf carry propane in all four tanks, whereas most leaving the Middle East have two tanks of propane and two tanks of butane, or three tanks of propane and one tank of butane. Thus, more VLGCs would be needed to carry the same volume of propane on that route versus the US Gulf–Asia route.

In addition, US cargo cancellations would lower propane prices, which could spur more spot cargoes, offsetting the shift to shorter-haul Middle East–China voyages. “You could get more cargoes going out of the US Gulf to Japan and South Korea, which takes a few days longer than to China,” Gray said.

In the dry bulk sector, tariffs implications are two-fold: the first involves steel and aluminium carried aboard Handysizes and Supramaxes, and the second involves argribulk aboard Panamaxes.

When implementing its first set of tariffs on 24 March, the United States temporarily exempted countries that accounted for almost 60% of last year’s steel imports and two-thirds of aluminium imports. Countries that were not exempted shipped 14.5 million tonnes of steel to the United States in 2017 and 2.5 million tonnes of aluminium. If exemptions expire in May, an additional 20 million tonnes of steel and 5 million tonnes of aluminium volume could be affected.

In the argribulk category, China has proposed a 25% tariff on US exports of soyabeans. According to preliminary data from the US Department of Agriculture, the United States exported 55.5 million tonnes of soyabeans last year, with 31.5 million tonnes, or 57%, shipped to China.

A tariff on US soyabeans could shift Chinese buying to Brazil. The voyage distance from Brazil to China is 10% longer than from Louisiana and more than double the distance from the US west coast argribulk port in Vancouver, Washington, a positive for vessel demand. In addition, there would be much more congestion at Brazilian ports, which would soak up tonnage.

Another major shipping segment facing possible tariff fallout is container shipping. Data from IHS-owned PIERS reveals the extent that US containerised import and export volumes are covered by tariffs and proposed tariffs. The United States imported a total of 22.9 million teu in containerised goods in 2017, of which 10.7 million teu, or 47%, came from China. The United States exported 12.5 million teu, with 2.7 million teu, or 22%, going to China, which was by far the largest destination for America’s containerised exports.

Products covered by the US steel and aluminium tariffs initially totalled 282,994 teu, or 1.2%, of US containerised imports in 2017. However, once exemptions were put in place, the affected products dropped to 212,324 teu, or just 0.9%, of imports.

The Chinese retaliatory tariffs against pork, fruits, nuts, aluminium scrap, wine, and steel piping covered product categories that accounted for 85,000 teu of outbound US cargoes in 2017, representing only 0.7% of total box exports. Thus, the initial tariffs by the United States and the Chinese response will have a minimal effect on trans-Pacific volumes, particularly given that tariffs do not necessarily erase the affected shipping demand – the cargo owner could pay the tariff and ship it anyway, or redirect the cargo to another country.

Under the USD50 billion in proposed US tariffs announced in response to alleged IP theft, PIERS data shows that affected categories accounted for 706,872 teu of Chinese exports in 2017. That represented 3.1% of all US containerised imports last year, and 6.6% of US box imports from China.

China’s proposed USD50 billion in retaliatory tariffs, which cover soyabeans, meats, and other perishable products, affect items that represented 180,098 teu of US shipments last year. This equates to 1.4% of total US containerised exports and 6.7% of US box exports to China.
