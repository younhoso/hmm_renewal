Project Logistics -
Hidden Helpers in
the Plant Industry

Project
Logistics -
Hidden
Helpers in the
Plant Industry

Jungyub Lee (HMM HQ)

Anyone would have thought of this when hearing news or reading newspaper articles that introduce gas production stations or offshore wind generators in the middle of the sea: “How did they move such a heavy generator into the middle of the sea?” The answer lies in project logistics. The plant industry refers to infrastructures or production facilities of industry, such as power generation, fresh water generation, oil refinery, petrochemical, port and environmental facilities. As the civilization of mankind becomes more sophisticated, the importance of project logistics is increasing day by day.

Potential of the plant industry and project logistics

Korea's EPC companies are a powerhouse in the plant industry, boasting the world's fifth-largest sales in overseas construction. Between 2007 and 2014, they recorded larger order volumes than those of the shipbuilding and semiconductor industries and achieved cumulative orders totaling about 770 billion dollars. In relation to this, the number of workers dispatched overseas had reached 145,000 (from 1978 to 2017). Korea’s EPC companies have been a pillar that has supported the Korean economy. As long as mankind does not reduce its reliance on energy, the project logistics market will continue to develop. In the Middle East and North Africa (MENA), an area which is called the oil field of the world, there are still several hundred trillion dollars of potential projects. Recently, emerging developing countries, such as Vietnam, India, and Thailand, are explosively developing, and this will be a new opportunity for the EPC industry.

Projects ordered in the Asia region accounted for 35% of the total, at US$139.9 billion this year alone and have already surpassed the Middle East to become the number one player in the global project market. Although the reputation of the EPC industry is not as good as it was in the past decade due to the global recession, the potential of the project logistics market is endless. Therefore, there will be plenty of opportunities to increase again.

Know-how necessary for transporting project cargo

Project logistics refers to the transportation and supply of all materials and heavylift cargo needed to carry out a project, such as a plant, to a site schedule. The main shippers are EPC (engineering, procurement and construction) companies1. Due to the nature of overseas orders the transportation method mainly includes sea transportation, and it involves forwarding, engineering, shore transportation, transportation in third party countries, unloading, installation and construction. Project logistics requires sophisticated technology and know-how for shore and sea transportation because the cargo itself is heavy and expensive and it has to operate without any problems even after delivering them to the site.

However, the project transportation business is more concentrated in ensuring stability, because project materials cannot be replaced in a short period of time when they are broken, causing the entire project to be delayed. Therefore, since 1970, when project orders from the Middle East began increasing explosively, the transportation industry, with heavylift ships optimized for project cargo, has developed along with these trends.

Heavylift ships can be distinguished from ordinary ships due to the appearance of cranes on board. While gantry cranes at shore terminals can only load about 50 to 60 tons of cargo, cranes installed on heavylift ships can load cargo from 100 to 2,000 tons, making it possible to safely load and unload huge weighty project materials and to work in various environments. As the ship is equipped with a crane on board, it can carry out cargo operation at sea and handle various cargoes even at ports that do not have infrastructure facilities.

Another advantage of a heavylift ship is that it has a rectangular shape with the inner walls of the cargo hold2 at right angles, unlike dry bulk ships. This provides an environment in which project cargo can be secured in a more reliable manner. In addition, since pontoons can be installed in cargo holds, it is possible to make two or three additional floors so that cargo can be safely shipped without any collision or stacking.

Project logistics integrated with expertise and sophisticated technology

More important than heavylift ship specifications in project cargo transportation is the experience of a shipping company. Since project cargoes consist of various structures, weights, and sizes for refinery, petrochemical, power generation, and oil gas, many accidents occur during a cargo operation process, which can affect the entire project. For this reason, expertise specialized in project cargo is emphasized at all sites.

Everything starts from a cargo design plan initially delivered by a shipper. A cargo design plan should be drawn up as precisely as possible so that a cargo superintendent can design a correct stowage plan because an inaccurate plan can make shipping impossible if the actual measurements are larger or heavier.

When a shipper delivers a cargo design plan, cargo superintendents and engineers draw up a stowage plan and a method of statement (shipment plan) indicating which method and equipment cargo is to be loaded or unloaded, where inside the cargo holds cargo will be placed, and how cargo will be secured to the ship based on this plan.

This is based on engineering knowledge rather than relying solely on the experience of cargo superintendents. Therefore, sophisticated engineering IT systems are used. European engineering companies initially designed and introduced heavylift ships. Since then, engineering technology has been the core and most essential technology for operating heavylift ships.

Based on this plan, cargo superintendents are dispatched directly to a site to supervise whether port stevedores, a ship’s crew and cargo lashers properly perform their work based on a stowage plan. Experience at each site directly affects the stability of cargo and the profitability of a shipping company. Their expertise directly influences the service quality of a heavylift shipping company, which is a competitive edge over a ship's specifications and freight competitiveness.

HMM’s project transportation service and its current status

The heavylift division of HMM began in 2007, while the world's first heavylift carrier was created in the 1920s. Although HMM has only 10 years of history, it has now become the largest heavylift carrier in Korea and beyond this, it has grown into a solution provider that is now comparable to European heavylift carriers. HMM’s four 640 Type ships, which mean that they can load or unload cargo with a crane capable of accommodating up to 640 tons, transport more than 50% of the heavylift cargo produced in the domestic EPC industry. In recent years, it has been jointly operating one AAL ship (700 Type) by joining a strategic alliance with AAL, the third largest heavylift carrier in the world, and plans to add one additional ship next year.

HMM has focused on “perfecting what it can do well” through a choice and concentration strategy over the past decade. Instead of unreasonably operating many routes, HMM has operated a liner service in the Middle East and the Red Sea route, concentrating on these regions, which is unusual for a heavylift carrier. Through this, HMM has steadily grown its network and experience in the barren Middle East ports and provided differentiated services from European shipping companies, thus having grown the “heavylift business of HMM as one brand.”

Since HMM’s heavylift business has always been able to ensure their customers' trust that they will be guaranteed HMM’s transportation schedule and cargo stability while keeping their promise to customers, HMM’s heavylift service has become, in 10 short years, one of its masterpieces.

1EPC (engineering, procurement and construction) companiesEPC is an English initial acronym of engineering, procurement and construction. It refers to a business in which a company which has acquired a large-scale construction project or an infrastructure contract provides design, parts and materials procurement and construction on a one-stop basis.

2cargo holdA cargo hold refers to a place where cargo can be loaded that a ship owner provides shipping service to a shipper
