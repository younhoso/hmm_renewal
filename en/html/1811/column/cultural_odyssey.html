Cultural Odyssey

Addictive Fitness: Behind the World’s Healthiest New Obsession

Addictive Fitness: Behind the World’s Healthiest New Obsession

By Tim Alper(Journalist)

It is the news that doctors everywhere were waiting for: obesity rates everywhere are starting to fall. Rather than slouching on their couches in front of the TV, modern urbanites are instead joining the fast-developing global fitness revolution – swapping junk food for jogging meetups, and trading goof-off time for gym classes. Experts once fretted that globalization and modern technology might make us increasingly inactive. Quite the opposite has turned out to be true.

Exercise has become more diverse, and more accessible. Gyms used to be the exclusive haunts of muscle-bound sorts, grunting heavily as they pumped gigantic weights. They were not easy to find, and provided an inhibitive environment for anyone without a bodybuilder physique or scores of ultra-keen gym buddies.

But things have changed. Now, if you walk down the street in almost any major global city, you will likely pass a whole plethora of gyms. Many of these venues explicitly aim to make exercise a less anti-social experience. Interactive GX and dance classes are now the norm. Tailored, personal training sessions – once the preserve of the super-rich or super-devoted – have become more affordable and accessible than ever.

Fighting Fit

The globally interconnected nature of life in the late 2010s has also helped niche exercise methods spread across the planet like wildfire. Yoga is almost as old as civilization itself, and has been practiced in what is now India for millennia. Yet in the past few years, its popularity has exploded worldwide. A survey by Yoga Journal found that over 36 million Americans practiced yoga as of 2016 – a massive jump from a lowly 4 million in 2001.

Yoga is not alone – a far more modern fitness system is also winning converts with the same breathless alacrity. Developed by a German émigré to the United States and then the UK, Pilates also emphasizes balance and core strength, but incorporates all sorts of equipment and accessories that yoga tends to do without. Pilates studios are now almost as ubiquitous as yoga centers and gyms – and adherents are every bit as committed (and evangelical) as the most hardened of yoga fiends!

Yoga, Pilates and other fitness routines have benefitted greatly from the contemporary social media scene. In the past, making an exercise video was a mammoth chore, requiring a large crew and hours’ worth of filming. Now all you need is a smartphone, 10 minutes of your time, a tripod and an internet connection. Creating a fun, short, interactive, shareable workout video is the best way for teachers to advertise their services – or monetize their expertise via ad revenue on video sharing platforms.

Park Life

The running scene, which used to be pretty much a closed-shop boys’ club for rake-thin expert long-distance athlete types, has also exploded dramatically – again thanks to contemporary IT. Parkrun began as a dozen people jogging around a London park on a Saturday morning. Thanks to the internet, it has now grown into an international phenomenon, with 5 million members in 20 countries, including Russia, Malaysia and Swaziland.

The phenomenon has won the support of British medics, with the Royal College of General Practitioners now encouraging doctors to advise overweight patients to take up Parkrun. Some 400 family medicine clinics in the UK have signed up to become certified Parkrun practices.

In Korea, where running clubs were once dominated by middle-aged men, social media and chat apps have helped a whole generation of cool young city-dwellers establish “running crews,” informal gatherings of hyper-trendy bright young men and women, who run along city riversides sporting the trendiest exercise gear around. Their blend of fitness and fashion yields quite dazzling results.

The upshot of all this workout diversity is that exercise in 2018 is no longer an intimidating chore. Rather, it has become an exhilarating and welcoming way of life – with the potential to make us all a whole lot healthier!

By Tim Alper(Journalist)

Tim Alper is a British journalist who has lived in Seoul for 11 years. He has contributed to publications such as The Guardian, The Jewish Chronicle, Joongang Ilbo, Weekly Chosun and Korean Air’s Morning Calm. He is also the author of the book Bananas & Couscous and the co-author of Have Fork, Will Travel.
