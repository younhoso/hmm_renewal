About Bulbous Bow

Basri Ay / HMMA

My name is Basri Ay, with the HMMA office. Having earned a bachelor’s degree in naval architecture and marine engineering, I have built several vessels with bulbous bows, and as we all work for an ocean carrier, I thought that it would be beneficial for the HMM family to get more familiar with vessels and their structure/machinery. This was my motivation to prepare this article, which I hope you enjoy.

Have you ever wondered about the nose like structures at the very tip of vessels and what are they for? They are called bulbous bows. The bulbous bow decreases water resistance around the hull, to help vessels in increasing speed and fuel efficiency. The bulbous bow should be designed carefully; if not, despite its intended purpose, it may increase drag and could cause more fuel consumption.

Bulbous bows can be incredibly efficient depending on the size of a vessel, and are generally used in large hull surface vessels such as tankers, bulk carriers, container vessels, and so forth. That is why bulbous bows are not seen in small crafts such as yachts. There are of course exceptions, such as mega yachts, which are more like commercial vessels than leisure boats

Bulbous bow technology is fairly new in the history of seafaring, and since the first designs and applications were seen in the early 1900s, they have undergone considerable advancement.

The first examples of use of bulbous bows are seen in military vessels such as the USS Delaware launched in 19091, 2, followed by commercial ships such as the SS Europa, a passenger ship launched in 19283. Today they are widely utilized by many different types of vessels.

In addition to their primary use, bulbous bows can also be used for special purposes like ice breaking. For example, they have been used in the service vessels of the arctic seas to break the ice and to open sea paths, as well as to rescue ships that are stuck in the ice waters.

Bulbous bows are designed through sophisticated mathematical calculations and with special computer programs, and tested in special basins with a scaled ship model under realistic sea and wave conditions. Once the resistance and powering performance testing results are found satisfactory and efficient, the bulbous bow is approved and then incorporated for building into vessels in shipyards as per the approved designs.

To see the real results, of course, sea trials are also critical before final delivery. If the specific speed/fuel consumption requirements are not met, then the bulbous design should be re-evaluated. However, as there are so many factors affecting the speed and fuel consumption of a vessel such as hull design, weight distribution, the propeller type used and even the paint applied on the hull, it is important to conduct a comprehensive review.

You can find some examples of “nose jobs” done on our vessels as well. The bulbous bow conversion of the first HMM vessel, the Hyundai Brave, was completed in March 2013. Test results after the installation found that the bulbous bow helped reduce fuel consumption by almost 1,000 tons per year4. I hope this article has assisted you in understanding the real-life usage of bulbous bows.
