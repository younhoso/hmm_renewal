Life Goes On

Tokyo, the Cultural Melting Pot of Japan

Jungyub Lee / HMM HQ

I recently had the opportunity to visit Tokyo, Japan, capital to the land of the rising sun. Since I was born in Seoul, Korea and raised in Hong Kong, I am very used to major Asian cities, but Tokyo was an experience unique unto itself.

One of the standout features of Tokyo is that you can find so much tradition within such a technologically advanced city. Take for example, the 400-year-old Asakusa shrine which reflects the beauty of Japanese culture and architecture of the Edo-period. While traditional buildings found across Northeast Asia might look the same to people of the western world, they are clearly very different. For example, Korea’s traditional architecture aims for harmony with nature with structures designed to be simpler and smaller to blend in to the natural surroundings, while Japanese architecture is more “people” oriented.

The splendor and massiveness of the Asakusa shrine emphasizes the power of the holder, with buildings fancier and bigger than those you will find in Seoul. The gigantic lantern on the entrance greets your visit and the five-story wooden pagoda, which is as tall as a modern building, completes the authentic atmosphere. You can also find the surrounding streets filled with tradition, and there are numerous stalls selling food, as well as stores, well over 100 years old, selling different kinds of souvenirs.

If Asakusa represents traditional Japanese culture, you can experience modern Japanese culture at Akihabara, which is one of the most developed districts in Tokyo. Akihabara represents the soft power of modern Japan, and is filled with video games, animations, comics and other aspects of otaku culture. You will find the streets filled with shinning neon signs and a whole building selling newly released games and game machines or character goods from infamous Japanese animations and comics. Even if you might not be a big fan of modern Japanese culture, the features of Akihabara are so unique you may spend hours going from building-to-building, and shop-to-shop to see the master craftsmanship of the Japanese.

During my visit to Japan, every place I visited was a mind-blowing experience, but my favorite was the night view at Roppongi Hills Observatory. Hong Kong, where I grew up is also famous for its beautiful night view, but I was mesmerized by the view I saw at the observatory. You can see a massive city filled with lights, the Tokyo tower flowing with color, and infinite buildings closely adjoined to each other. If the night view of Hong Kong can be represented by its colorful lights, Tokyo’s can be represented by its absolute and massive scale.

By looking at the night view, I realized why so much people are attracted to the city of Tokyo. Immensely and rapidly developed after accepting western culture, but still maintaining its traditions, the city is a unique cultural melting pot. If you haven’t visited Tokyo before I personally recommend that you visit, since it is a unique experience that you won’t see anywhere else.
